SignRefine: Adapting Foundational Video Models for Sign Language Generation

Anton Pelykh1,  Ozge Mercanoglu Sincan1,  Edward Fish1,  Richard Bowden1

1CVSSP, University of Surrey, United Kingdom

Paper arXiv Code

Abstract

Sign language video generation demands precise hand and facial articulation, yet modern video diffusion models, trained predominantly on spoken-language video, produce artifacts that render signing unintelligible. We propose SignRefine, a sign language video generation model that produces comprehensible signing from 2D keypoint conditioning alone, generalizing across appearances and visual conditions. Our approach builds on a pretrained video diffusion transformer and introduces local adapters with spatial grounding to selectively refine hand and face regions, steering the strong base model's prior toward accurate articulation. To enable this work and support broader sign language research, we present NVSign, the first large-scale dataset of television content natively produced in sign language, offering diverse signer appearances, dynamic visual environments, and natural conversational settings. Trained on this data, our model shows up to 30% improvement in hand pose precision metrics over the strongest baseline and is preferred by sign language users for visual quality and comprehensibility in more than 80% of comparisons.

Contributions

Generalizable Pose-to-Video Generation The first generalizable pose-to-video generation approach designed to improve the comprehensibility of generated sign language content across diverse signer appearances, scales, and visual conditions.
Local Adapters with Spatial Grounding for DiT Backbones A novel conditioning scheme for DiT-based human video models using local adapters with spatial grounding, enabling targeted refinement of hand and face areas without degrading the quality and generalizability of the frozen backbone.
NVSign — First Large-Scale Natively Signed Dataset Introduction of NVSign, the first large-scale, multi-modal natively signed broadcast television dataset with 2,184 unique signers, 80.5 hours of video, and dynamic multi-party conversational scenes absent from all prior corpora.
Rigorous Quantitative and Perceptual Evaluation Through evaluation on NVSign and Phoenix14T test sets, and a user study with 15 BSL users of varying proficiency, our approach achieves up to 30% improvement in hand pose precision (MPJPE) and is preferred in over 80% of pairwise comparisons over all baselines.

Method

Given a reference image defining signer appearance and a full-body keypoint sequence, SignRefine generates a video depicting faithful hand articulations and facial expressions. The system is built on Wan2.1-1.3B-Fun-Control, a pretrained video DiT, kept frozen while trainable local adapters steer it toward precise articulation.

Frozen DiT Backbone Wan2.1-1.3B-Fun-Control conditions on full-body skeleton sequences using a T5 text encoder, CLIP image encoder for reference appearance, and a 3D VAE for latent video encoding. The backbone remains frozen during training, preserving generalizable visual priors while adapters steer toward accurate sign articulation.
Regional Conditions with Spatial Grounding (§4.1) Face, left hand, and right hand are processed by three independent ResNet-style condition encoders on 256×256 regional crops, downsampled to a 16×16 feature grid. Absolute normalized coordinate maps (CoordConv) are appended to each crop, embedding crop-to-frame geometry directly into regional features to resolve spatial ambiguity.
Localized Feature Injection with Sink Tokens (§4.2) Motion vectors are injected via masked cross-attention into selected DiT blocks. A trainable sink token absorbs background queries, preventing adapter features from bleeding into unrelated regions. Face adapter injects at blocks {0, 6, 12, 18, 24}; hand adapter at {2, 8, 14, 20, 26}, distributing conditioning across the 30-block network.
Spatially Weighted Adapter Training (§4.3) MSE loss with non-uniform spatial weighting: hand and face regions weighted 10× over background (wh = wf = 10, wb = 1), concentrating gradient signal on articulation regions critical for sign comprehension. Trained on ~40K clips for 30K iterations on 4 NVIDIA GH200 GPUs in bfloat16 precision.

NVSign Dataset

NVSign is the first large-scale dataset of broadcast-quality television content natively produced in sign language, created by Deaf producers and actors. Unlike existing datasets that rely on interpreter signing in fixed studio settings, NVSign captures sign language in its most authentic form — with dynamic camera angles, diverse environments, and natural multi-party conversational scenes that push the field beyond isolated sentence translation.

Scale & Diversity 2,184 unique signers · 80.5 hours of high-resolution video · 48,972 scenes · 317 episodes across 114 distinct shows · British Sign Language (BSL)
Conversational Scenes Approximately 40% of the dataset features two or more participants in natural conversational settings — the only large-scale sign language corpus with this property — enabling study of multi-signer dynamics, turn-taking, backchanneling, and non-manual discourse markers.
Dynamic Cameras & Native Signing Features moving and switching camera angles rather than fixed studio viewpoints. Content signed natively by Deaf performers eliminates structural biases from spoken-language interpretation and captures expressive, unrestricted natural signing.
Multi-Modal Annotations Time-aligned English transcripts, dominant topic clusters, per-signer activity scores, extracted 2D full-body keypoints (RTMPose-X), depth maps, SMPL body, MANO hand, and FLAME face parameters. Three data partitions for nuanced model evaluation.

User Study

A perceptual study with 15 BSL users of varying proficiency (basic to advanced) evaluated naturalness, visual quality, and semantic fidelity. Participants completed 17 questions (Visual Ranking and Text Reference Ranking). Our model achieves the lowest mean rank overall (1.22 vs. 1.89 for the next-best Wan-FC) and is preferred head-to-head over every baseline — in more than 83% of pairwise comparisons, with near-unanimous dominance over SignGAN and SignViP.
